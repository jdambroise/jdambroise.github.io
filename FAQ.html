FAQ and Affirmative Rephrasing Answer
"Calculus isn't really used for anything, so why should I learn it?"

BETTER:

"How is Calculus used in real life?"
Calculus has many applications, and we learn about them in Calculus class! Come to class, read the book and engage in lively discussion with me and your peers.
"There is too much memorization in math. How is that real learning?"

BETTER:

"Are there any advantages to memorization?"
To have basic facts at your fingertips is a powerful tool for being creative on a higher level. On the other hand, it is understandable to feel overwhelmed if a large portion of the material is very new to you. Get the help you need, start early, and work hard. Furthermore, there are tricks and tips that we will frequently talk about in class for how to remember certain formulas/methods. The better you understand the reasoning behind the material, the less likely you are to characterize "knowing stuff" as "memorization".
"I've always been good at math until this course."

BETTER:

"How is this class different than other math classes I have taken?"
As you move up in the math world, you are expected develop more independent knowledge and we will be carving out new and interesting parts of your mathematical brain. Past success is not an indicator of future success -- and it shouldn't be! You should expect that this class will challenge you in new and different ways.
"My past math courses didn't contain trigonometry, so I can't do this problem."

BETTER:

"How can I learn the needed trigonometry so that I can do this problem?"
Either take a refresher course or work hard to meet regularly with someone who can help you (me, a tutor, a math-savvy peer, etc.).
"I didn't understand the lecture, can you teach me everything in office hours?"

BETTER:

"I studied this or that from the book this weekend, but I still have some gaps in understanding what we covered today. Can we meet to talk about it?"
One of the most important things in this class is to develop the right amount of independence balanced with the right amount of support -- and that, my friends, has no formula.

To get more out of lectures, read the book, prepare in advance, and most importantly ask questions during class as much as possible! When all that fails and things are still not making sense, communication is the key. Come by office hours and I will help you sort out any issue, small or large.
"I couldn't make it to class yesterday, did I miss anything?"

BETTER:

"I must miss class tomorrow for an excusable reason and I checked the syllabus regarding what we will cover. I also plan on obtaining a copy of a peer's notebook. Can you provide me with any more information about tomorrow's class?"
Since we do cover new material each class, you are right to plan in advance. The most important thing you will miss is the dynamic act of witnessing the mathematics being explained and worked out explicitly for your benefit. So, don't make a habit out of missing class. If missing class is an absolute must, work hard to catch up and come to office hours to clear up any needed points.
"I know the answers to the homework but the online entry is too picky. Will you grade my homework by hand?"

BETTER:

"How can I learn the correct syntax for entering mathematical expressions into the online homework system?"
Learning how to deal with mathematics and technology is part of the course material! Seek help at the math help center, come to office hours, and feel free to email me shorter syntax questions. If you would like more feedback on the written aspect of figuring out the homework problems, this is a great reason to come by office hours, stop by the math help center, and/or work with peers. I frequently meet with students to give pointers about organizing work and understanding the flow of logic. These meetings can be highly productive since they are on an individual level.
"I forgot the homework was due, can I have an extension?"

BETTER:

"I have notated the due dates of future assignments in my calendar, what are the policies on late assignments?"
Homework deadlines are strict. You can submit questions for reduced credit after the deadline.
"Why do we have to do long problems?"

BETTER:

"What should I be focusing on learning from long problems?"
When you tackle long, multi-step problems, you are learning to stay organized and methodical. This will help your overall efficiency, and you will improve at both longer and short problems! When you train to be good at something, your training should push you far and force you to consider complex problems.
"I didn't know there was a quiz last week, can I take a makeup?"

BETTER:

"I understand that my lowest quiz score will be dropped, how can I stay better informed about future quiz dates?"
Come to class, read the syllabus, check the google calendar, and if still in doubt don't hesitate to ask me for confirmation.
"I know this material but I just don't do well on exams."

BETTER:

"How can I understand this material in such a way that I might do better on exams?"
There is a difference between recognizing a pre-computed/provided solution is correct, versus being able to construct such a solution on your own with just a pencil and an empty sheet of paper. The former relies on other people to do all or part of the work for you, and the latter constitutes the type of well-internalized and independent understanding we want to cultivate in this class. A great way to improve your performance is to test yourself by attempting problems with a blank sheet of paper and no resources -- that's pretty much what the exam will be like ! Can you still do the problem under these circumstances?
"I know this material but silly mistakes keep adding up."

BETTER:

"How can I stop silly mistakes from adding up?"
Everyone makes mistakes including myself. There will always be small mistakes here and there, and it is important not to beat yourself up over it. On the other hand, if you are making enough small mistakes to significantly add up to effect your grade, likely you are rushing, not rechecking answers, or you may simply not have enough background in prerequisite material. You can improve by slowing down, being careful, studying/ practicing more, working on improving your efficiency, testing yourself at home, and rechecking your answers during exams.
"I know this material but the test was too long and I would have done much better with more time."

BETTER:

"How can I improve my efficiency in taking exams?"
By the nature of exams themselves, yes, you can expect to be tested on a wide range of topics from the course. This is why it is important to practice efficiency in figuring out problems. Often when students are spending much longer than expected periods of time doing exams, it tends to be because they are trying out numerous different methods to solve a problem. It is a good thing to have a bunch of mathematical methods at your fingertips, but even better is to be able to recognize quickly which method is required. Spending less time debating various methods shows that you have mastered the material at a high level. This is the type of mastery we want to work on developing in this class. Create daily study habits, read the book, ask questions frequently, prepare in advance, test yourself in exam-like scenarios etc..
"I know this material but that test question really caught me off guard."

BETTER:
"How can I obtain sample test questions?"
Homework and quiz problems are sample test questions, and you can find a larger catalogue of these questions in the book. Would you still know how to do these problems if I changed the function? the interval? the numbers? These are a few examples of how I write exams based on quizzes and homeworks.
"Why aren't grades scaled?"

BETTER:

"What is your methodology behind the grading scheme?"
Exams are a random selection of course material that you are expected to have mastered, and I write exams expecting A-performing-students to earn an A, B-performing-students to earn a B, etc.. In other courses you may come across differing expectations. For example, in a course I once took as a student, A-students were expected to get about 30% of the exam questions correct. This is because the professor preferred to ask new and unexpected questions that the students had never seen before. This works well for a particular type of teaching style, but I prefer not to teach this way. It's just not my style.
"I studied for hours and did poorly on the exam. Can I do extra credit?"

BETTER:

"Can you help me improve my studying efficiency so that I may do better on the next exam?"
Studying is an extended activity that when done correctly takes much more devotion than one single multi-hour pre-test study session. The most effective way to prepare for exams is to create daily and weekly consistent study habits spanning the entire semester. If you have done this and are still experiencing problems with performance, seriously consider whether you are mathematically prepared to take this course and/or whether you are in need of more substantial extra help in identifying your mathematical weaknesses. Remember that the earlier you address the crux of the issue and the more help you seek, the better chance you will have of doing well. These are the types of conversations I love having in office hours because it gives me a way to really help you concretely !
Come by and we can talk about how the course is going for you !
"My other teachers structured the course some other way, you should do it that way too."

BETTER:

"What is the reasoning behind the way you structure the course?"
I base the course structure on what I have learned works best for me and for my students over the years. This is a continually evolving process! I am happy to hear about new ideas for course structure and activities, but changing the overall structure mid-semester is not a good idea. Keep in mind that I may have tried something similar to your idea in the past, or I may be thinking about how I can enhance your idea for the best result next semester. I welcome your feedback but also ask for your understanding in realizing that not all ideas are immediately implementable.
"I don't agree with exams philosophically, so I shouldn't have to take them."

BETTER:

"How can I participate in exams knowing that philosophically I disagree with them?"
There are indeed numerous times in life when we must compromise in order to work with others. Taking my exams does not mean you are conforming to my tyrannical rule, and my insistence on administering exams does not mean I am disrespecting your philosophical views. I fully support differing teaching styles, differing philosophies / ideologies / ways of life etc.. At the end of the day I must do my best to make decisions that I feel will work in my classroom, and I use my best judgment in deciding how to structure the course.
"I am not doing well because I have a job or other obligations. Can I do some other work to make up for it?"

BETTER:

"Can we have a conversation about whether this course fits into my life plan?"
Yes, please come by and speak with me about whether this course fits into your overall plans -- this semester and otherwise! When you commit to being a student, that is also part of your job. Some students begin taking this course but switch out for a more suitable course, and others take on the course material knowing that they will need to work hard learning prerequisite material. What works for you is highly individual and I am fully supportive of any scenario where you do what is best for you ! In fact, having such conversations with students is my favorite part of this job so feel free to come by.
